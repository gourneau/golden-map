Published in Sagan, Sagan & Drake, \"A Message from Earth,\" Science 175(4024):881–884 (1972). The Voyager Golden Record cover (1977) reuses the identical map (NASA: \"the pulsar map previously sent as part of the plaques on Pioneers 10 and 11\"), so the data epoch is ~1969–1971, not 1977.\n- Geometry: 15 lines radiate from a single point (the Sun). 14 point to pulsars; the 15th — the longest, carrying no binary number — runs to the Galactic Center and sets both the angular reference and the distance scale. Each pulsar line's LENGTH is the pulsar's in-plane distance relative to the Sun–GC distance; a short TICK at each line's end encodes the z-offset perpendicular to the galactic plane, making the map 3D.\n- Period encoding: along each pulsar line, a binary number gives the pulsar's spin period as a multiple of the hydrogen 21-cm hyperfine transition period. Exact unit: 7.04024183647e-10 s (= 1/1,420,405,751.77 Hz; ~0.704024 ns — NOT 0.70452 ns; NASA rounds to \"0.70 billionths of a second\"). decoded_period_s = binary_value × 7.04024183647e-10.\n- Binary convention: vertical stroke = 1, dash = 0; most significant bit first (at the Sun end; LSB at the outer end of the line). THE ALIEN DECODING PROBLEM\n\nDecoding chain a finder must execute: (1) recognize the hydrogen diagram → adopt 0.704024 ns as the unit; (2) read tick/dash binary (deduce MSB-first from all-leading-1s); (3) multiply to get 14 periods; (4) match against their pulsar catalog after integrating spin-down backward over an unknown elapsed time; (5) correct each period for light-travel time from pulsar to Earth (hundreds to tens of thousands of years, distances ~0.08–8 kpc); (6) triangulate from angles + lengths + z-ticks; (7) rewind proper motions and the Sun's ~230 km/s galactic orbit to find where the Sun WAS at launch.\n\nQuantitative degradation timeline (composite of Sagan/Drake 1972, NatGeo/Ransom 2021, Siegel 2017, canonical pulsar lifetimes):\n- Now–~1 Myr: essentially intact; all 14 detectable and matchable. (Already after ~50 yr the periods are measurably stale — the Smithsonian floor map re-encodes them for a 2026 epoch.)\n- Several Myr: ordinary pulsars begin fading (typical radio lifetime ~10 Myr, range 10–100 Myr; ~99% of neutron stars ever born no longer pulse); NatGeo/Ransom: the map's pulsars \"fade and disappear within several million years.\" Beam alignment/precession removes beacons from any given sightline even earlier; young high-P-dot pulsars (Crab, Vela) drift fastest; 3.75-s B0525+21 sits nearest the death line.\n- Tens of Myr: designers' own optimistic bound (\"few tens of millions of years\") reached; most signposts gone.\n- ~100 Myr–1 galactic orbit (200–250 Myr): galactic shear destroys the geometry outright; identification also fails as 9-digit periods of a changing quantity stop being unique keys against ~10^8–10^9 Galactic neutron stars.\n- A Ransom-style replacement using millisecond pulsars + globular clusters would stay readable ~1 Gyr.\nSpacecraft context: Voyager 1 ~172.6 AU (Mar 2026), first human object one light-day out ~Nov 2026, passes 1.7 ly from Gliese 445 (AC+79 3888) in year 40,272; Voyager 2 ~142.5 AU, passes ~1.7 ly from Ross 248 in ~40,000 yr and ~4.3 ly from Sirius in ~296,000 yr.
